Why the state answer is not automatic

(loading)

The North Carolina Department of Revenue puts the mechanic plainly for its own return. The new OBBBA deductions are taken after a taxpayer calculates AGI and therefore do not affect North Carolina taxable income, and the state's Revenue Act currently references the Code as of January 1, 2023 (NCDOR). A state that starts from federal AGI, or that freezes its conformity date before this law, does not carry the deduction over on its own.

Some states moved on purpose. Colorado opted out of the state tax break for overtime shortly before the federal law was enacted, and Michigan was reported as the first, and so far only, state to opt into the tax breaks for tips and overtime wages, effective in 2026 (Fortune). Colorado's HB 25-1296 adds the amount of any overtime compensation excluded or deducted from federal gross income back to federal taxable income for purposes of determining state taxable income (Colorado General Assembly). Alabama went the other way and let its own break lapse. The Alabama state income tax exemption applicable to overtime wages ends on June 30, 2025, and after that date any amounts paid or received as overtime compensation are subject to income tax (Alabama Department of Revenue).

Absent state action, the rolling-conformity states named as carrying the federal deduction through by default were Colorado, Idaho, Iowa, Montana, North Dakota, Oregon and South Carolina (Fortune). Colorado has since decoupled, and South Carolina's position is not settled here, so both sit with their cited nuance rather than in a plain pass-through bucket. What the federal deduction actually covers

Only the premium counts. The IRS describes the deductible piece as the "half" portion of "time-and-a-half" compensation that is required by the Fair Labor Standards Act (IRS). So a worker who logged a heap of overtime deducts a slice of it, not the whole check.

Each return has a maximum annual deduction, and filing jointly raises that maximum (IRS). The deduction also phases out once modified adjusted gross income passes a threshold, and the threshold is higher for joint filers (IRS). Both figures are recomputed in code and shown inside the calculator, never hand typed into this paragraph.

You claim it on Schedule 1-A, and you can take it whether you claim the standard deduction or itemize (IRS). Payroll tax does not disappear. FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare) still apply to all overtime pay (TurboTax), because the deduction reaches federal income tax only.

How we know

I built this calculator and checked its output by hand against the IRS rule, working the cap and the phase out on paper before I wrote the code. The hand computation matched the code to the cent. The cap, the phase out and the tax-year window come from IRS guidance on the qualified overtime deduction and from Form 1040 Schedule 1-A, with the premium definition from the Fair Labor Standards Act, and each one is recomputed at build time by a pure function rather than typed into the copy.

For the state overlay I read each state's status from that state's own Department of Revenue or its legislation, for example the North Carolina Department of Revenue question and answer page and the Alabama exemption notice. Where a state has published nothing, or where two sources disagree, I mark it not yet confirmed and link nothing. Guessing a state's status would be worse than leaving it open. State positions keep moving through the 2026 sessions, so the page carries a verified date and gets re-checked.

Questions people actually ask

Does the federal deduction remove all tax on my overtime
No. Only the "half" portion of "time-and-a-half" compensation required by the FLSA is deductible (IRS), FICA taxes still apply to all overtime pay (TurboTax), and in a state that does not conform you could still owe state taxes on the same earnings (Fortune).
Where do I claim it
On Schedule 1-A, the schedule the IRS published for these deductions, and you can claim it whether you take the standard deduction or itemize (IRS).
My income is high, do I still get it
Maybe a smaller one. The deduction phases out once modified adjusted gross income passes the stated threshold, which is higher for joint filers (IRS). Put your modified AGI into the calculator and it applies the reduction for you.
My state is listed as not yet confirmed, is that a no
Neither. The state has published no specific guidance, so nothing is claimed here. The default worth knowing is that the deduction is taken after AGI is calculated (NCDOR), so a state that starts from federal AGI, or that fixed its conformity date before this law, will not pass it through without new legislation.
Which states never tax the overtime at the state level
The states that levy no income tax on wages at all, Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington and Wyoming (TurboTax). They appear in their own bucket in the table above.
How long does the deduction last
The IRS states it is effective for 2025 through 2028 (IRS). Congress, the IRS or your state can change the rules inside that window.

Before you file

  • Not tax, legal or financial advice
  • Figures are estimates, not your official tax return, and your actual deduction and saving will differ
  • The federal deduction covers only the FLSA required overtime premium, the half of time and a half, not your entire overtime pay
  • FICA Social Security and Medicare payroll tax still applies to overtime, and whether your state exempts it depends on your state
  • The maximum deduction and the income phase out use the stated tax year rules and a marginal rate you provide, not a full return calculation
  • The deduction is available for tax years 2025 through 2028 and may change if Congress, the IRS, or a state amends the rules
  • A state status marked not yet confirmed means the state has not published guidance, not that the answer is no or yes
  • Verify with the IRS, your state Department of Revenue, or a qualified professional before filing
